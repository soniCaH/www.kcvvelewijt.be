Phase 6.B · Drill round 1 · `<MatchArticleLinkCard>`

How should the article link sit between the events and the related-articles?

Three treatment levels for the new <MatchArticleLinkCard>. Auto-hides if no matchPreview / matchRecap article exists for the match. All three render the finished state (linking to a recap); the upcoming state is identical in shape, only the badge / kicker copy differs.

Pick one treatment level → write d4 lock
Variant A

Compact one-line link.

Single inline row: kicker pill on the left, article title in the middle, ellipsis arrow on the right. No image. Minimum visual weight; reads as an in-text pointer. Focus stays on lineup + events above.

Pros

  • Smallest possible footprint — reader's attention stays on the match facts
  • Zero new primitive (one styled link + a small mono kicker pill)
  • Works identically on mobile without layout changes

Cons

  • Low click affordance — easy to scroll past
  • Doesn't sell the editorial content; the article author's effort gets less visual weight than the BFF lineup data
  • Title-only — no lead to preview the recap content
Variant B

Hero-style cover card.

Full-width <TapedCard> with the article cover image bleeding to the top edge. Kicker + heading + one-line lead below the image. Same shape as <NewsCard> on the homepage — the strongest CTA of the three options.

Pros

  • Strongest "go read this" affordance — image + kicker + heading + lead + CTA
  • Surfaces editorial work prominently; rewards article authors
  • Composes existing primitives 1:1 (<TapedCard> + <TapedFigure> + <EditorialHeading>)

Cons

  • Visually indistinguishable from a homepage <NewsCard> — may confuse readers ("why is there a news card in the middle of the match page?")
  • Dominates the body — competes with the match content for attention
  • Mobile: adds a full-width image block, more scroll
Variant C

Inline magazine banner.

Mid-weight horizontal card. Small square cover image (~120px) on the left, kicker + title + lead on the right, arrow at the far right. Reads like a magazine sidebar callout — compromise between A's restraint and B's prominence.

Pros
