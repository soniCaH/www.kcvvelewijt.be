PHASE 2 · TRACK A · DIRECTION C ★ ALERT · BANNER STRIP 2026-04-30

Banner strip.

A bold ink-filled header band sits at the top of the alert carrying INVERSE mono caps title — the icon and the variant label live in the band, the body content sits below in a tinted soft fill. Sharp corners, no rotation, no gradients. The alert is still a paper card (border-2 ink + shadow-paper-sm) — the band lives INSIDE that card. Feel: fanzine breaking-news strip — loud, ink-emphasized, demands attention. This direction is the most opinionated about urgency.

★ 01 · STANDALONE VARIANTS — success · warning · error · stacked at full width

Drie strips, drie urgenties.

Three semantic variants share one chassis. The band colour does the talking — jersey-deep for emphatic-friendly, mustard ochre for "look here", dusty brick for "now". Body fills follow as the soft-tinted sibling of each band. Title is mono caps, INVERSE cream-on-band.

★ 02 · WITH TITLE + DISMISSIBLE — inline close button on the band's right edge

Sluitbaar op de strip.

The dismiss control lives on the band, not in the body — a 22×22 cream outline button with a hairline cream border, hovering to inverted (cream fill / ink mark). It belongs to the band's chrome, not the content. Title is always present in this scale; the strip is loud enough that an unlabelled icon-only mode would feel dishonest.

★ 04 · TRADEOFFS — why this direction wins · what it sacrifices

Waarom strip, en wanneer niet.

↑ FOR

Most opinionated about urgency of the three Track A directions — alerts read as proper urgent communications, not generic notices. The ink-filled header strip is the loudest "LOOK HERE" signal in the system without resorting to gradients or animation. INVERSE mono caps title carries fanzine breaking-news energy and ties directly into BrandedTabs / FilterTabs / Spinner active-state vocabulary (cream-on-ink). Three semantic colours (jersey-deep / warning / alert) read at a glance even from across the page. Sharp corners + paper-card chassis keep it native to Direction D.

↓ AGAINST

Risks feeling loud at low information density. A short success message ("Verzonden!") gets the same theatrical banner treatment as a critical failure — the strip can feel over-committed for routine confirmations. Stacked vertically (e.g. multiple form errors) the repeated dark bands carry visual weight that competes with the surrounding editorial. The two-row vertical structure (band + body) also takes more height than a single-row inline alert, which may feel heavy in dense dashboards or admin tables. Mitigation: the inline scale is materially shorter, and the variant restraint (only 3 colours) prevents the strip from becoming visual noise across a page.
